Foundation & Focus

Phase 1

Scheer's Core Focus: Cloud Mindset & Best Practices

Scheer's authority does not start with visibility, tools, or messaging techniques. It starts with focus. Scheer exists for one reason only: to make end-to-end business processes work in SAP Cloud ERP, using proven best practices rather than experiments. This is not a positioning choice. It is a structural constraint that governs every external and internal activity.

Enterprise ERP programs fail far more often due to process fragmentation, governance gaps, and unrealistic expectations than due to software limitations. Decision-makers at C-level know this instinctively. What they look for is not innovation rhetoric, but signals of discipline, realism, and experience. Scheer's irreducible core speaks directly to that need.

In practice, this means Scheer deliberately avoids conversations that drift toward feature comparisons, tooling depth, or innovation theater. Those topics create noise early in the buying journey and weaken trust. Instead, Scheer consistently reframes discussions toward how businesses actually operate across Order-to-Cash, Procure-to-Pay, Record-to-Report, and related end-to-end flows.

What typically goes wrong: Organizations underestimate the long-term cost of customization, overestimate the flexibility of public cloud ERP, and fail to recognize how quickly deviations from standard erode governance and auditability. Scheer's role is not to amplify optimism, but to surface these realities early and calmly.

Concrete applications:

  • In ERP replacement programs, Scheer starts with where process handovers break during transition periods, rather than with SAP functionality or roadmap discussions.
  • In multi-entity rollouts or carve-outs, Scheer explains how fit-to-standard is the only sustainable way to maintain comparability, control, and compliance across entities.
  • In compliance-driven transformations, Scheer frames value in terms of audit readiness, traceability, and predictable operations, not speed or innovation metrics.

This focus must be visible everywhere: in content, profiles, outreach, and conversations. If a message cannot be directly traced back to process integrity, governance, and predictability, it does not belong in the Scheer Authenticated Leadership Program.

Phase 2

Profile Design: Authority Through Evidence

In the Scheer Authenticated Leadership Program, profiles are not CVs, portfolios, or capability statements. They are structured evidence that Scheer can deliver predictable outcomes in complex ERP contexts. The objective is not to list experience, but to surface patterns, judgment, and decision-making frameworks that matter to program owners facing governance, compliance, or multi-entity complexity.

What typically goes wrong at this stage is premature visibility. Organizations invest heavily in polished LinkedIn profiles, personal branding, and content calendars before establishing what the profile actually signals. This creates inconsistency. A profile that emphasizes innovation, breadth, or generalist consulting sends the wrong signal for Scheer's focus. Authority in enterprise ERP does not come from being visibleâit comes from being the obvious choice when a specific, high-stakes problem arises.

Profile structure must reflect Scheer's constraint:

  • Current role description must connect directly to fit-to-standard execution in SAP Cloud ERP, not generic SAP expertise.
  • Experience summaries emphasize program complexity (multi-entity, compliance, carve-outs) rather than project volume or years of service.
  • Content and activity demonstrate process thinking, not product knowledge or feature updates.

Concrete examples of what works:

  • Instead of "15 years of SAP experience," use "Delivered 8 multi-entity S/4HANA Public Edition rollouts with clean-core discipline across retail and wholesale sectors."
  • Instead of "Certified SAP consultant," use "Specialized in Order-to-Cash and Procure-to-Pay process design under CSRD and SOX compliance constraints."
  • Instead of listing tools or certifications, describe the decisions made during program execution and how governance was maintained.

Profiles are not static. They evolve as consultants publish content, engage in outreach, and demonstrate consistent thinking over time. However, changes must be deliberate, not reactive. Authority is damaged when profiles shift tone, scope, or focus in response to trends or competitors.

Content Strategy: Earned Attention - Principles

What typically goes wrong at this stage is volume-first thinking.   • Constraint acknowledgment over optimism: Real programs operate under budget limits, timeline pressure, and organizational friction. Content that ignores these realities is not credible.
  • Decision frameworks over feature explanations: Buyers need to understand when fit-to-standard is the right choice, when extensions are justified, and how to evaluate trade-offsânot what SAP's latest roadmap contains.

What Scheer content is not:

  • Product updates, feature announcements, or SAP press releases repackaged
  • Innovation theaterâclaims about AI, digital transformation, or disruption without delivery evidence
  • Thought leadership that prioritizes cleverness over utility
  • Competitor comparisons or vendor debates

Content should be rare, deliberate, and substantive. Publishing weekly for the sake of visibility undermines authority. Publishing only when there is something material to sayâwhen a pattern has been observed across multiple programs, when a governance issue has been resolved in practice, when a trade-off decision framework can be sharedâbuilds credibility over time.

Content Strategy: Earned Attention - Application

Concrete content types that work for Scheer:

1. Post-mortem reasoning
Describe a recurring failure pattern (e.g., integration testing delays in multi-entity rollouts) and explain how governance structures prevent it. Do not name clients or projects. Focus on the decision logic, not the outcome.

2. Trade-off analysis
Explain when standard processes work, when extensions are justified, and how to evaluate the long-term cost of deviations. Decision-makers value clarity on constraints more than promises of flexibility.

3. Governance case studies
Describe how compliance requirements (CSRD, SOX, GDPR) shape process design in SAP Cloud ERP. Explain what standard delivers, what requires extension, and how auditability is maintained.

4. Process handover breakdowns
Surface where Order-to-Cash, Procure-to-Pay, or Record-to-Report processes fail during carve-outs, mergers, or entity consolidations. Explain how clean-core discipline prevents fragmentation.

Content should not be frequent, it should be inevitable. When a pattern becomes clear across multiple programs, when a trade-off framework has been validated in practice, when a governance structure proves sustainableâthat is when content is published. The cadence is dictated by experience accumulation, not editorial calendars.

Distribution discipline:

  • Content is published on LinkedIn as the primary channel (where decision-makers conduct research)
  • No cross-posting to multiple platforms simultaneously (signals desperation for reach)
  • No hashtag optimization, engagement bait, or algorithmic gaming
  • No promotional language, company branding, or capability statements in content body
  • Responses to comments must maintain the same tone and discipline as the original content

Content that earns attention creates inbound interest. When buyers recognize Scheer's patterns in their own programs, they reach out. That is the signal of authorityâbeing found when needed, not being visible when convenient.

Outreach Mechanics: Qualified Conversations - Principles

In the Scheer Authenticated Leadership Program, outreach is not lead generation. It is qualification in reverseâScheer determines whether a conversation is worth having before the prospect does. The objective is not to maximize meetings, responses, or pipeline volume. It is to initiate conversations only with decision-makers who face complexity that Scheer's focus can address.

What typically goes wrong at this stage is scale-first thinking. Organizations prioritize outreach volume, response rates, and automation efficiency before establishing what qualifies as a meaningful conversation. This creates misalignment. A high response rate from companies that lack multi-entity complexity, governance constraints, or fit-to-standard discipline requirements is not progressâit is noise.

Outreach must be constraint-driven:

  • Only contact organizations where Scheer's focus applies: Multi-entity structures, compliance requirements (CSRD, SOX, GDPR), carve-out situations, or failed ERP programs with governance gaps.
  • Only reach out to decision-makers who own process outcomes: CFOs, COOs, transformation leaders, or process ownersânot IT directors, project managers, or procurement.
  • Only initiate when there is signal of readiness: Program announcements, compliance deadlines, organizational changes, or public statements about ERP challenges.

What disqualifies a prospect:

  • Single-entity organizations with simple operations (Scheer's value does not apply)
  • Companies with no governance, compliance, or auditability requirements
  • Organizations already satisfied with customization-heavy ERP strategies
  • Prospects who prioritize innovation, speed, or lowest cost over predictability

Qualification is not rejection. It is discipline. Scheer's authority is damaged when consultants engage in conversations where fit-to-standard discipline is irrelevant or unwelcome. A smaller pipeline of qualified conversations is always better than a large pipeline of mismatched prospects.

Phase 5 Part 1

Rhythm & Discipline: Consistency Without Urgency - Cadence

In the Scheer Authenticated Leadership Program, rhythm is not activity volume. It is predictable presenceâthe pattern that emerges when focus, profiles, content, and outreach operate consistently over time without urgency, pressure, or reactive behavior. Authority is not built through intensity. It is built through inevitability.

What typically goes wrong at this stage is mistaking visibility for authority. Organizations prioritize daily posting, aggressive outreach cadences, and campaign sprints, expecting momentum to create credibility. This backfires. In enterprise ERP contexts, urgency signals inexperience. Buyers interpret frequent content, fast follow-ups, and promotional intensity as desperation, not confidence.

Scheer's rhythm operates on constraint, not ambition:

  • Content is published when patterns are clear, not when calendars dictate. If three months pass without substantive material, that is acceptable. Forcing weekly content to maintain visibility damages authority.
  • Outreach happens when qualification is confirmed, not when pipeline targets require activity. A consultant who initiates five qualified conversations in a quarter is more valuable than one who sends 200 generic messages.
  • Profile updates reflect real experience accumulation, not optimization tactics. Adding a new multi-entity program to a profile is meaningful. Rewording the same experience for algorithmic reach is not.

What rhythm is not:

  • Daily LinkedIn activity, engagement loops, or comment campaigns
  • Weekly content quotas, posting schedules, or editorial calendars
  • Automated follow-up sequences, nurture campaigns, or drip marketing
  • Reaction to competitors, trends, or market noise

Rhythm means decision-makers see Scheer's thinking surface regularly enough to recognize patterns, but infrequently enough to signal deliberation. When content appears, it is substantive. When outreach happens, it is relevant. When profiles update, they reflect real delivery.

Concrete discipline practices that maintain rhythm:

Do not optimize profiles for algorithms, trends, or competitor positioning. Authority comes from what the profile signals over years, not months.

5. Internal accountability
Rhythm is maintained through peer review, not individual autonomy. Before content is published, profiles are updated, or outreach is initiated, consultants validate that the activity reinforces Scheer's irreducible core. If it does not, it is delayed or discardedâregardless of how much effort was invested.

Phase 6

Acceleration Layer: Tools Without Loss of Control

In the Scheer Authenticated Leadership Program, tools are never the strategy. They are an acceleration layer applied only after focus, profiles, content, outreach, and rhythm are firmly in place. Tooling exists to support disciplined execution, not to replace judgment, experience, or accountability.

What typically goes wrong at this stage is tool-first thinking. Organizations introduce automation, AI, dashboards, or platforms too early, expecting efficiency to compensate for unclear positioning or weak discipline. This leads to faster inconsistency, not better outcomes. In enterprise ERP contexts, loss of control is interpreted as risk.

Scheer takes the opposite approach. Control always stays with consultants and leadership. Tools are introduced selectively and transparently, with a clear boundary: automation may support drafting, tracking, or reuse, but it must never make decisions, shape positioning, or initiate contact autonomously.

Acceleration is only valuable when it reinforces what already works. AI can help structure content, summarize patterns, or prepare first drafts, but thinking remains human. Dashboards can track cadence and consistency but never replace qualitative judgment. Content libraries can enforce message discipline but never encourage copy-paste behavior without context.

Concrete application examples:

  • AI drafting: Used to create content outlines based on known ERP patterns, after which an expert rewrites and validates every claim.
  • Dashboards: Monitor whether cadence and rhythm are maintained over time, not how many views or reactions were generated.
  • Prompt libraries: Ensure consistent framing while responsibility for final output remains with the author.
  • Constraint: If a tool introduces speed at the expense of clarity, restraint, or accountability, it does not belong in Scheer ALP. Acceleration without control is regression.

In this phase, restraint is a strength. The objective is not to do more, but to do the same right thingsâmore consistently, with less friction.

Common Pitfalls & Prevention

Even with clear discipline, organizations implementing the Scheer Authenticated Leadership Program can drift toward patterns that undermine authority. These pitfalls are predictable and preventable.

Pitfall 1: Volume-First Thinking
Symptom: Weekly content quotas, aggressive outreach cadences, daily LinkedIn activity.
Consequence: Urgency signals inexperience; decision-makers interpret frequency as desperation.
Prevention: Track qualified conversations initiated, not messages sent. Publish only when patterns are validated. Gaps between activities are evidence of discipline, not failure.

Pitfall 2: Tool-First Acceleration
Symptom: Introducing AI, dashboards, or automation before focus and content discipline are established.
Consequence: Faster inconsistency; loss of control damages authority.
Prevention: Deploy tools only after Phases 1-5 are stable. Ensure humans validate all output and retain decision-making authority.

Pitfall 3: Reactive Positioning
Symptom: Shifting profiles, messaging, or content in response to competitor activity, trends, or market noise.
Consequence: Authority is damaged when positioning becomes unstable.
Prevention: Anchor all activity to Scheer's irreducible core. Ignore trends, competitors, and innovation rhetoric. Update only when real experience accumulates.

Pitfall 4: Premature Outreach
Symptom: Contacting prospects before qualification is confirmed, sending generic messages, or following up aggressively.
Consequence: Misaligned conversations waste time and weaken Scheer's positioning.
Prevention: Qualify rigorously before outreach. Surface constraints first, pitch capabilities never. Disqualify and move on when fit is unclear.

Pitfall 5: Engagement Optimization
Symptom: Using hashtags, tagging influencers, asking for comments, or optimizing for LinkedIn algorithms.
Consequence: Signals promotional intent; buyers disengage when content feels like marketing.
Prevention: Publish content without engagement tactics. Let substance drive discovery. Authority is earned through utility, not visibility.

Pitfall 6: Innovation Theater
Symptom: Content about AI, digital transformation, disruption, or SAP feature announcements without delivery evidence.
Consequence: Credibility erodes when claims cannot be validated through real program outcomes.
Prevention: Write only about patterns observed across multiple programs. Avoid topics that require speculation or promotion.
